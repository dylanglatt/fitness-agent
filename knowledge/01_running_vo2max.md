# Running Performance, Speed Development & VO2max Training

## 1. VO2max — What It Is and Why It Matters

VO2max is the maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during intense exercise. It is the single strongest predictor of endurance performance and one of the most powerful biomarkers of long-term health and longevity. It is measured in mL/kg/min.

**Reference ranges:**
- Untrained male: 35–40 mL/kg/min
- Recreational runner: 45–55 mL/kg/min
- Competitive age-grouper: 55–65 mL/kg/min
- Elite male runner: 70–85 mL/kg/min

VO2max is trainable. The greatest gains come from high-intensity interval training (HIIT), supplemented by high-volume easy aerobic work (zone 2). The two are not mutually exclusive — they work synergistically.

**Primary drivers of VO2max improvement:**
1. Cardiac stroke volume (how much blood the heart pumps per beat)
2. Mitochondrial density in muscle tissue
3. Capillary density (oxygen delivery to muscles)
4. Hemoglobin concentration
5. Muscle oxygen extraction efficiency

## 2. Heart Rate Zones for Running

Use 5-zone model based on max heart rate (MHR). Best way to find MHR: run a hard 5K all-out, or do a field test (10-min warmup, then 3 x 3-min all-out with 3-min recovery, take highest HR reached).

| Zone | % MHR | Feel | Purpose |
|------|--------|------|---------|
| Zone 1 | 50–60% | Very easy, conversational | Recovery, warm-up |
| Zone 2 | 60–70% | Easy, can hold full conversation | Aerobic base, fat oxidation, mitochondrial growth |
| Zone 3 | 70–80% | Moderate, short sentences only | Tempo, threshold development |
| Zone 4 | 80–90% | Hard, 1–2 words only | Lactate threshold, race pace |
| Zone 5 | 90–100% | Max effort, unsustainable | VO2max intervals, speed |

**Key insight:** Most recreational runners spend too much time in zone 3 — the "gray zone" that is too hard for aerobic adaptation but too easy for VO2max stimulus. This is the single biggest mistake.

## 3. Polarized Training (80/20 Rule)

Pioneered by researcher Stephen Seiler studying elite endurance athletes across all sports. Elite performers universally do approximately 80% of training volume at low intensity (zones 1–2) and 20% at high intensity (zones 4–5). Very little time is spent in zone 3.

**Why this works:**
- Low intensity builds aerobic base, mitochondria, fat oxidation, and cardiac efficiency without accumulating fatigue
- High intensity provides the VO2max and speed stimulus
- Zone 3 creates fatigue without providing adequate stimulus for either adaptation

**Practical application for Dylan:**
- If running 4x/week: 3 easy runs (zones 1–2), 1 hard workout (zones 4–5)
- Easy runs should feel almost embarrassingly easy — you should be able to hold a full conversation
- HR cap for easy runs: approximately 70% of max HR (e.g., if MHR = 190, keep under 133 bpm)

---

## 4. MAF Method (Maffetone Aerobic Training)

Developed by Dr. Phil Maffetone. Establishes a maximum aerobic heart rate for easy running:

**MAF HR = 180 − age**

For a 30-year-old: MAF HR = 150 bpm. All easy running is done at or below this number.

**Adjustments:**
- Subtract 5 if recovering from illness, injury, or overtraining
- Add 5 if training consistently for 2+ years with no illness or injury

Training at MAF for 3–6 months builds aerobic base. Progress is measured by pace at MAF HR — as fitness improves, you run faster at the same low heart rate.

## 5. Lactate Threshold Training

The lactate threshold (LT) is the intensity at which lactate begins accumulating in the blood faster than it can be cleared — typically around 85–90% of MHR or roughly 10K race pace.

Training the LT allows you to sustain faster paces before fatigue sets in. This is the primary target of "tempo" running.

**LT workout types:**
- **Sustained tempo:** 20–40 min continuous at LT pace (comfortably hard — can say 3–4 words)
- **Cruise intervals:** 4–6 x 8–10 min at LT with 90-second recovery
- **Threshold repeats:** 3 x 15 min at LT with 3-min recovery

**LT pace estimation:** Approximately 25–30 seconds per mile slower than 5K race pace.

---

## 6. VO2max Interval Training

The most effective workouts for raising VO2max. Done at zone 5 / ~95–100% max effort.

**Classic formats:**
- **4 x 4 min at VO2max pace, 3-min recovery** — the "Norwegian" model, research-validated by Helgerud et al.
- **5 x 3 min hard, 3-min easy**
- **6–8 x 800m at 5K race pace**
- **Tabata-style:** 8 x 20 sec all-out, 10 sec rest (less effective for trained athletes)

**Frequency:** No more than 1–2 VO2max sessions per week. These sessions are high fatigue — WHOOP recovery must be green (67%+) before attempting.

## 7. Speed Development

Speed for distance runners is built through:

**Strides:** 4–6 x 20-second accelerations at the end of easy runs. Not sprints — controlled accelerations to ~90% effort. Build neuromuscular efficiency. Do 2–3x/week.

**Track workouts (specific formats):**
- 400m repeats at mile pace (6–12 reps, 90-sec recovery)
- 200m repeats at 5K effort (10–16 reps, 60-sec recovery)
- 1-mile repeats at 10K effort (4–6 reps, 90-sec recovery)
- Ladder: 400m–800m–1200m–800m–400m at 5K pace

**Hills:** Hill repeats develop power and speed with lower injury risk. 6–10 x 30-sec hard uphill, walk down recovery.

## 8. Running Economy

Running economy is how efficiently you use oxygen at a given pace — essentially your "mpg." Better economy means faster running at lower HR.

**What improves running economy:**
- **Cadence:** Target 170–180 steps/minute. Low cadence (under 160) increases impact forces. Use a metronome app to train this.
- **Strength training:** Heavy squats, deadlifts, plyometrics all improve running economy significantly (research shows 5–8% improvement)
- **Form:** Forward lean from ankles (not waist), land under center of mass, relaxed shoulders and arms
- **Mileage:** Simply running more over time improves economy

## 9. Aerobic Base Building

The foundation of all running performance. Cannot be shortcut.

**Rules for base building:**
- 10% rule: increase weekly mileage no more than 10% per week
- Every 4th week: reduce volume by 30–40% (recovery week)
- 80%+ of all running at easy/zone 2 intensity during base phase
- Duration: 8–16 weeks of dedicated base building before adding intensity

**Signs your aerobic base is developing:**
- Pace at same easy HR improves (faster for same effort)
- RHR drops
- WHOOP HRV trends upward over weeks

---

## 10. Progressive Overload for Running

| Variable | How to Progress |
|---------|----------------|
| Volume | Add 1–2 miles/week to long run, or add 1 easy run |
| Intensity | Increase interval speed or duration |
| Density | Reduce recovery times between intervals |
| Frequency | Add a run day |

**Safety rules:**
- Never increase volume AND intensity in the same week
- Take a recovery week every 3–4 weeks
- If WHOOP recovery is red (<34%) for 2+ consecutive days: reduce load immediately

---

## 11. WHOOP-Guided Running Decisions

| Recovery Score | Training Intent |
|---------------|----------------|
| 67–100% (Green) | Hard workout appropriate — intervals, tempo, or long run |
| 34–66% (Yellow) | Easy run only, keep HR in zones 1–2 |
| 0–33% (Red) | Rest or 20–30 min walk only |

**HRV context:** If HRV is trending down over 5+ days even while recovery scores are yellow/green, treat it as an overreaching signal. Reduce intensity for 3–5 days.

**RHR context:** Elevated RHR (+5 bpm above baseline) signals illness, stress, or accumulated fatigue. Do not run hard when RHR is elevated.

## 12. Race-Specific Training Protocols

**5K (8–10 week plan structure):**
- Week 1–3: Aerobic base + strides
- Week 4–7: VO2max intervals + tempo runs
- Week 8: Taper (reduce volume 40%, keep intensity)

**10K (10–12 week plan):**
- Heavier emphasis on lactate threshold work
- 3–4 tempo runs per mesocycle
- VO2max intervals 1x/week

**Half marathon (12–16 week plan):**
- Long run builds to 12–14 miles
- Race pace work (sustained miles at goal HM pace)
- Less high-intensity, more volume and threshold work

## 13. Common Mistakes That Slow Runners Down

1. **Running easy runs too fast** — the #1 mistake. If you can't hold a conversation, you're in zone 3.
2. **Running hard runs too easy** — intervals should be genuinely hard, not "kind of hard"
3. **Increasing mileage too fast** — leads to injury before adaptation
4. **Skipping strength training** — big performance and injury-prevention mistake
5. **Neglecting recovery** — training is the stimulus, recovery is when adaptation happens
6. **Ignoring WHOOP data** — pushing through red recovery days accumulates debt
7. **No structure** — random running without workout types doesn't optimize adaptation
8. **No long run** — the long run is the cornerstone of endurance development
9. **Poor fueling** — running long distances without carbohydrate fueling impairs adaptation
10. **Doing the same workout every week** — variety of stimuli is required for continued progress
