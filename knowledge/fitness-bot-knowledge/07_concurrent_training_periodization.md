# Concurrent Training & Periodization

## 1. The Interference Effect — Real but Manageable

Concurrent training (combining endurance and strength training in the same program) creates what is known as the "interference effect" — endurance training can blunt some strength and hypertrophy adaptations when both are performed in close proximity.

**The mechanism:**
- Endurance training activates AMPK (energy-sensing enzyme) — signals the body to prioritize mitochondrial biogenesis and fat oxidation
- Strength training activates mTOR — signals the body to prioritize muscle protein synthesis
- AMPK suppresses mTOR: When activated together, endurance work partially blunts the strength signal

**How significant is it?**
- Meta-analysis (Wilson et al., 2012): Concurrent training reduces strength gains by ~8–10% and hypertrophy by ~7–10% compared to strength-only training
- For recreational athletes (not competing in strength sports), this is largely irrelevant
- Smart programming eliminates most of the interference effect

**Key finding:** The interference effect is primarily a problem when running and lifting are done in the same session or within 6–8 hours of each other, especially with lower body emphasis.

## 2. Session Sequencing — Order Matters

**Same day, two-a-day sessions:**

| Scenario | Order | Notes |
|----------|-------|-------|
| Heavy lift day + short easy run | Lift first, run later (6+ hours) | Minimal interference |
| Hard run day + upper body lift | Run first, lift after 3+ hours | Lower body not impaired |
| Heavy leg day + any run | Do NOT combine — separate days | Maximum interference risk |
| Easy run + full body lift | Lift first, run later | Better for strength goal |
| Upper body focus | Either order | Minimal lower body conflict |

**General rule:** When both sessions are the same day, prioritize the quality you care more about — do that first when energy and neural drive are highest.

## 3. Optimal Weekly Schedule for Running + Lifting

**The principle:** Hard running days should not be adjacent to lower body lifting days. Upper body lifting can happen any time relative to running with minimal interference.

**Sample Week A (Balanced — Dylan's profile):**
```
Monday:    Upper body lift (heavy)
Tuesday:   Hard run (intervals or tempo)
Wednesday: Lower body lift
Thursday:  Easy run (zone 2) + optional upper body accessory
Friday:    Rest or easy run
Saturday:  Long run
Sunday:    Rest or mobility
```

**Sample Week B (Strength-focused):**
```
Monday:    Upper body lift
Tuesday:   Easy run + lower body accessory
Wednesday: Lower body lift (heavy)
Thursday:  Easy run
Friday:    Upper body lift
Saturday:  Long run
Sunday:    Rest
```

**Sample Week C (Run-focused):**
```
Monday:    Easy run + upper body lift
Tuesday:   Hard run (intervals)
Wednesday: Full body lift (moderate)
Thursday:  Easy run
Friday:    Rest
Saturday:  Long run
Sunday:    Easy run
```

**Key rules:**
- Heavy leg day → no hard run within 24 hours before or after
- Long run → no lower body lift within 24 hours after
- Hard run + lower body lift same day: 8+ hours minimum separation, and this should be rare

## 4. Managing Cumulative Fatigue Across Modalities

Running and lifting create different types of fatigue that can stack:

**Peripheral fatigue:** Localized muscle damage and glycogen depletion. Recovers with rest, nutrition, and sleep.

**Central nervous system (CNS) fatigue:** Neural fatigue from high-intensity efforts. Recovers in 48–72 hours. Both heavy lifting and hard running create CNS fatigue.

**Signs of cumulative fatigue (check WHOOP):**
- Recovery trending down despite normal training load
- RHR elevated above baseline
- Performance declining in both running and lifting
- Unusual soreness extending beyond 72 hours

**Managing it:** Track weekly strain on WHOOP. If weekly strain consistently exceeds your norm for 2+ weeks without adequate recovery days, you are accumulating fatigue debt.

## 5. Periodization — The Science of Planned Progress

Periodization is the systematic organization of training over time to maximize performance while managing fatigue.

**Why periodize:**
- The same training stimulus produces the same adaptation → stagnation
- The body needs varying stimuli (volume, intensity, type) to continue adapting
- Planned recovery periods allow supercompensation (coming back stronger)
- Prevents overtraining by building in recovery

---

## 6. Types of Periodization

**Linear periodization:** Progressively increase intensity over weeks/months while decreasing volume. Classic model. Works well for beginners and intermediate athletes. Predictable and easy to follow.

Example (12-week strength):
- Weeks 1–4: 3x12 @ 70% 1RM (volume phase)
- Weeks 5–8: 4x8 @ 75–80% 1RM (strength phase)
- Weeks 9–12: 5x5 @ 82–88% 1RM (peaking phase)

**Undulating periodization (DUP):** Vary the training stimulus week to week or day to day. Avoids accommodation faster than linear. Well-suited for concurrent athletes.

Example:
- Monday: Strength focus (5x5 @ 85%)
- Wednesday: Hypertrophy focus (3x12 @ 70%)
- Friday: Power/speed focus (4x6 @ 75%, fast tempo)

**Block periodization:** Dedicated training "blocks" each emphasizing one primary quality. Popular with advanced athletes.

- Block 1 (4 weeks): Accumulation — high volume, lower intensity (base building)
- Block 2 (4 weeks): Intensification — lower volume, higher intensity (strength/speed)
- Block 3 (2–3 weeks): Realization/peaking — low volume, max intensity or race prep

---

## 7. Training Structure — Macro, Meso, Microcycles

**Macrocycle:** Full training year or season. May contain 2–4 mesocycles and a competition period.

**Mesocycle:** 4–8 week training block with a specific focus (base building, strength, speed, maintenance).

**Microcycle:** Individual week of training — the smallest repeated unit.

**Annual planning for Dylan (runner + lifter, no specific race goal):**
- January–March (12 weeks): Base building — high easy run volume, moderate lifting volume, habit building
- April–June (12 weeks): Strength + speed development — introduce intervals, increase lifting intensity
- July–September (12 weeks): Performance — race or fitness events, peak performance window
- October–December (12 weeks): Maintenance + recovery — reduce volume, maintain fitness, mental reset

## 8. Deload Weeks — The Most Skipped Variable

A deload week is a planned reduction in training load (volume and/or intensity) to allow full recovery and supercompensation.

**When to deload:**
- Scheduled: Every 3–4 weeks (hard/hard/hard/deload pattern)
- As-needed: When WHOOP shows 5+ consecutive days below 50% recovery
- Before key events: 1–2 weeks before a race or major competition
- After an illness or travel week

**How to deload:**
- Reduce total volume (sets) by 40–50%
- Maintain exercise selection and intensity (keep weights similar — just fewer sets)
- Keep at least 1 easy run to maintain movement
- Do NOT attempt PRs during deload

**Common mistake:** Skipping deloads because you "feel fine." Deloads work on a cellular level independent of subjective feeling. The adaptations from previous weeks are consolidated during deload.

| Basketball (pickup) | 14–18 | Moderate–High |
| Squash (competitive) | 16–20 | High |
| Tennis (singles) | 13–17 | Moderate–High |

**How to account for sports:**
- Day before a sport: avoid heavy lower body lifting
- Day of sport: treat as a hard training day (no additional hard training)
- Day after basketball/squash/tennis: treat as the day after a hard run (easy or rest)
- Check WHOOP strain after the sport session — it will quantify exactly how hard your body worked

## 10. WHOOP-Guided Autoregulation

Using daily WHOOP data to make training decisions removes guesswork. This is "autoregulation" — adjusting training to biological readiness rather than a fixed plan.

**Decision framework:**
| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Green recovery + planned hard workout | Execute as planned |
| Green recovery + planned easy workout | You could push harder if desired |
| Yellow recovery + planned hard workout | Downgrade to moderate (70–80% effort) |
| Yellow recovery + planned easy workout | Execute as planned |
| Red recovery + any workout planned | Easy only, or rest |
| HRV trending down 5+ days | Reduce overall load for 3–5 days |
| RHR elevated 5+ bpm | Treat as illness signal — rest or easy only |

**Strain targets by goal phase:**
- Base building: Target strain 12–15/day on hard days, 6–9 on easy days
- Peaking: Allow strain 16–19 on hard days (brief periods only)
- Maintenance: Average strain 10–13/day across the week

## 11. Minimum Effective Dose — Maintaining Fitness

During busy periods, travel, or life disruptions, a minimum effective dose maintains fitness:

**Running maintenance:**
- 2 runs/week (one easy, one harder/long) maintains >90% of aerobic fitness for 4–6 weeks
- Below 2 runs/week, aerobic fitness begins declining measurably

**Lifting maintenance:**
- 1x/week per muscle group maintains strength for 8–12 weeks
- 2x/week maintains hypertrophy adaptations indefinitely

**Key insight:** It takes far less training to maintain fitness than to build it. During life disruptions, 2 short sessions per week is vastly better than stopping entirely.

## 12. Overreaching vs. Overtraining

| | Functional Overreaching | Non-Functional Overreaching | Overtraining Syndrome |
|--|------------------------|----------------------------|-----------------------|
| Duration | 1–2 weeks | 2–6 weeks | Months |
| Recovery time | Days | Weeks | Months–years |
| WHOOP signal | Temporarily suppressed | Consistently low | Severely suppressed |
| Performance | Temporary decline | Sustained decline | Severe decline |
| Action required | Reduce load 3–5 days | Deload 1–2 weeks | Complete rest + medical |

**WHOOP red flags for overreaching:**
- HRV >15% below personal baseline for 5+ days
- Recovery score <35% for 7+ consecutive days
- RHR elevated 5+ bpm for 5+ days
- Performance declining in running (slower at same HR) and lifting (weights feel heavier)
