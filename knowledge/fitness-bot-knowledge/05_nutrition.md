# Nutrition & Fueling for Performance and Body Composition

## 1. Macronutrients — Roles and Optimal Amounts

**Protein (4 cal/g):**
- Builds and repairs muscle tissue
- Most satiating macronutrient
- Highest thermic effect (25–30% of calories burned in digestion)
- **Target for active athletes:** 0.7–1.0g per pound of bodyweight
- For a 175 lb person: 120–175g/day
- Distribute across 4–5 meals/snacks of 30–50g each for optimal muscle protein synthesis

**Carbohydrates (4 cal/g):**
- Primary fuel source for high-intensity exercise (running, lifting, sports)
- Stored as glycogen in muscle and liver
- The only macronutrient that significantly affects performance at high intensities
- **Target:** Varies by training load (see carb periodization below)
- Brain also runs primarily on glucose — low carb = cognitive impairment

**Fats (9 cal/g):**
- Primary fuel at low intensities (zone 1–2 running)
- Required for hormone production (testosterone, estrogen)
- Fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K)
- Essential fatty acids (omega-3s) are anti-inflammatory
- **Minimum:** 0.3–0.5g per pound of bodyweight to support hormonal health
- **Sources:** Olive oil, avocado, nuts, fatty fish, eggs

## 2. Carbohydrate Periodization

Match carbohydrate intake to training demand. Not all days require the same fuel.

| Training Day | Carb Target | Examples |
|-------------|------------|---------|
| Hard/long run or heavy lift | 2.5–4g/kg bodyweight | Rice, pasta, oats, sweet potato, fruit |
| Moderate training | 1.5–2.5g/kg bodyweight | Moderate portions of above |
| Easy/recovery day | 0.5–1.5g/kg bodyweight | Vegetables, small amounts of starch |
| Rest day (fat loss phase) | 0.5–1g/kg bodyweight | Mostly protein and fats |

**Why this works:** On hard days, carbs fuel performance and aid recovery. On easy days, the body can use fat as primary fuel, supporting fat loss without compromising adaptation.

## 3. Pre-Workout Nutrition

**For running (especially >45 minutes):**
- 1–2 hours before: balanced meal with 50–75g carbs + 20–30g protein + low fat
- 30–45 min before (if short on time): easily digestible carbs (banana, toast, sports drink)
- For morning runs <60 min: fasted is fine; for longer morning runs, eat carbs first

**For lifting:**
- 1–2 hours before: 30–40g protein + 30–60g carbs + low fat
- 30 min before: optional — caffeine (3–6 mg/kg bodyweight), creatine (5g), fast carbs

**Caffeine timing:** Consume 30–60 minutes before training for peak effect. Enhances strength, endurance, and fat oxidation. Avoid within 6–8 hours of sleep (half-life is 5–6 hours).

## 4. Post-Workout Nutrition

**The "anabolic window" — what's real:**
- The window is real but wider than previously thought — not 30 minutes, more like 2–3 hours
- If you ate a protein-containing meal 2–3 hours before training, post-workout nutrition is less urgent
- If fasted training or more than 4 hours since last meal — prioritize post-workout meal

**Optimal post-workout meal:**
- 30–50g high-quality protein (whey, chicken, eggs, fish)
- 50–100g fast-digesting carbs (white rice, potato, fruit) to replenish glycogen
- Low fat (fat slows absorption — fine for later meals, not ideal immediately post)

**Post-run vs. post-lift:**
- Post-run: carbs are even more important (glycogen depletion is higher)
- Post-lift: protein priority is equal or greater

## 5. Hydration & Performance

Dehydration of even 1–2% of bodyweight measurably reduces performance:
- 1% dehydration: 5–10% reduction in aerobic performance
- 2% dehydration: 10–20% reduction in strength and power
- 3%+: significant cognitive impairment, heat illness risk

**Daily hydration target:**
- Minimum: 0.5–0.6 oz per pound of bodyweight
- For active athletes (Dylan): 0.7–1 oz per pound = 120–175 oz/day (approximately 1 gallon)
- More on heavy sweat days (hot weather, long runs)

**Electrolytes:** Sweat contains sodium, potassium, magnesium, chloride. Plain water alone doesn't replace these. For runs >60 min or heavy sweat sessions, add electrolytes (sodium in particular — 500–1000mg per hour of intense activity).

**Hydration and HRV:** Dehydration elevates RHR and suppresses HRV. Proper hydration directly improves WHOOP recovery scores.

## 6. Creatine — The Most Evidence-Backed Supplement

Creatine monohydrate is the most researched supplement in sports science with consistent evidence for both strength and endurance benefits.

**Benefits:**
- Increases phosphocreatine in muscle → more ATP available for high-intensity efforts
- 5–10% improvement in strength and power output
- 1–3% improvement in high-intensity endurance performance
- Enhanced muscle glycogen storage (relevant for runners)
- Emerging research on cognitive benefits and neuroprotection
- May enhance HRV and recovery in some individuals

**Protocol:**
- Loading phase (optional): 20g/day for 5 days (4 x 5g doses) — saturates muscles faster
- Maintenance: 5g/day indefinitely — no cycling needed
- Timing: With a carbohydrate-containing meal (enhances uptake via insulin)
- Form: Creatine monohydrate — no need for expensive alternatives (creatine HCL, kre-alkalyn, etc.)

**Side effects:** Minor water retention in first 1–2 weeks (intramuscular, not subcutaneous). Scale weight may increase 2–4 lbs. This is not fat.

## 7. Supplements With Real Evidence

| Supplement | Dose | Evidence Level | Benefit |
|-----------|------|---------------|---------|
| Creatine monohydrate | 5g/day | Very high | Strength, power, recovery |
| Omega-3 (EPA/DHA) | 2–3g/day | High | Anti-inflammatory, HRV, brain health |
| Vitamin D3 | 2000–5000 IU/day | High | Bone health, immunity, mood |
| Magnesium glycinate | 300–400mg before bed | Moderate-high | Sleep quality, HRV, muscle function |
| Caffeine | 3–6 mg/kg pre-workout | Very high | Performance, fat oxidation |
| Beta-alanine | 3.2–6.4g/day | Moderate | Reduces muscular endurance fatigue |
| Ashwagandha | 300–600mg/day | Moderate | Cortisol reduction, recovery |

**Supplements with weak/no evidence:** BCAAs (if adequate protein consumed), glutamine, testosterone boosters, fat burners.

## 8. Nutrition for Muscle Gain (Lean Bulk)

**Caloric surplus:** 200–300 calories above TDEE. Aggressive bulks (500+ surplus) result in excessive fat gain.

**Protein:** 0.8–1g/lb bodyweight. Do not reduce protein during a bulk.

**Carbohydrates:** Prioritize around training. Higher carb on training days, slightly lower on rest days.

**Rate of gain:** Expect 0.5–1 lb/week total weight gain. More than 1 lb/week likely includes significant fat.

**Lean bulk timeline:** 12–20 weeks is typical before taking a diet break or cutting phase.

## 9. Nutrition Around Sauna & Cold Plunge

**Pre-sauna:** Eat a light meal 1–2 hours before. Avoid heavy meals (blood diverted from digestion). Eat adequate carbs to maintain blood glucose during heat stress.

**Post-sauna rehydration:** 16–24 oz water + electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium). 
Add a small amount of carbohydrate and protein (yogurt + fruit, or protein shake) to support recovery if post-workout.

**Pre-cold plunge:** Stay fueled — cold exposure increases caloric burn and suppresses appetite. Don't go in depleted.

**Post-cold plunge:** Protein + carbs within 30–60 minutes if near training.

## 10. Foods That Support HRV & Recovery

**Anti-inflammatory foods:**
- Fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel): omega-3s — 2–3 servings/week
- Berries (blueberries, tart cherries): antioxidants reduce muscle damage post-exercise
- Leafy greens (spinach, kale): nitrates improve blood flow and running economy
- Beets: dietary nitrates — shown to improve VO2max and endurance performance
- Turmeric/curcumin: anti-inflammatory (take with black pepper for absorption)
- Dark chocolate (85%+): flavanols support cardiovascular health and reduce inflammation
- Extra virgin olive oil: oleocanthal has aspirin-like anti-inflammatory effect

**Foods that impair recovery:**
- Ultra-processed foods: pro-inflammatory, impair gut microbiome
- Excessive alcohol: suppresses HRV, disrupts sleep, impairs muscle protein synthesis
- Trans fats: systemic inflammation
- High-sodium processed food: elevates blood pressure and RHR

## 11. Alcohol — Quantified Impact on Performance

**Alcohol and muscle protein synthesis:**
- 1.5g/kg alcohol post-training reduces muscle protein synthesis by 37% even with adequate protein
- This means a 175 lb person consuming 4+ drinks after training significantly impairs muscle growth

**Alcohol and fat loss:**
- Alcohol is metabolized first (7 cal/g) — fat oxidation stops while alcohol is present
- Disrupts sleep → elevated cortisol → increased fat storage signal
- Increases appetite next day (ghrelin elevation)

**Alcohol and running:**
- Impairs glycogen synthesis post-run
- Dehydration accelerates muscle damage
- Blunts aerobic adaptation signals

**Practical guidance:** Performance athletes should limit to 1–2 drinks maximum, and not within 24 hours of key training sessions or races. Complete abstinence in the 48 hours before a race is the evidence-based recommendation.
