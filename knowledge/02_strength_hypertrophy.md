# Strength Training & Muscle Hypertrophy

## 1. The Three Mechanisms of Hypertrophy

Muscle growth (hypertrophy) is driven by three primary mechanisms:

1. **Mechanical tension** — The force placed on muscle fibers during resistance training. The most important driver. Occurs when muscles are loaded under stretch (think deep squat, full range bench). Heavy compound lifts provide maximum mechanical tension.

2. **Metabolic stress** — The "pump." Accumulation of metabolites (lactate, hydrogen ions) during higher-rep training. Creates hormonal environment favorable for growth.

3. **Muscle damage** — Microtears in muscle fibers that are repaired larger and stronger. Eccentric (lowering) portion of lifts creates most damage. Soreness is a proxy for this, but soreness is not required for growth.

**Key insight:** You need sufficient mechanical tension above all else. The other two mechanisms are secondary.

---

## 2. Progressive Overload — The Non-Negotiable Principle

The single most important concept in strength training. Your body only adapts to demands placed upon it. If the demand doesn't increase over time, adaptation stops.

**Methods of progressive overload (in order of preference):**
1. Add weight to the bar (e.g., bench 145 → 150 lbs)
2. Add reps at the same weight (e.g., 3x10 → 3x12 → add weight)
3. Add sets (e.g., 3 sets → 4 sets)
4. Reduce rest time (same work, shorter recovery = more density)
5. Improve range of motion or technique

**Practical rule for Dylan's chat logging:**
- If you hit the top of a rep range (e.g., 3x12) comfortably, add weight next session (5 lbs upper body, 10 lbs lower body)
- If you fail to complete reps, keep weight the same until you hit target reps

## 3. Volume, Frequency, Intensity

**Volume (sets x reps x weight):** The primary driver of hypertrophy. Research by Brad Schoenfeld establishes:
- Minimum effective volume (MEV): ~5–10 sets/muscle/week
- Optimal hypertrophy range (MAV): 10–20 sets/muscle/week
- Maximum recoverable volume (MRV): 20–30+ sets/muscle/week (individual variation)

**Frequency:** Each muscle group should be trained 2x/week minimum for optimal hypertrophy. Protein synthesis peaks ~24–48 hours post-training and returns to baseline by 72 hours. Hitting each muscle twice captures two synthesis peaks per week.

**Intensity:** For hypertrophy, train between 30–85% of 1RM. However, research shows that sets taken near failure (within 1–3 reps of failure, or RPE 7–9) are what matter most — not the exact load.

---

## 4. Rep Ranges

| Rep Range | % 1RM | Primary Adaptation |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|
| 1–5 reps | 85–100% | Maximum strength, neural efficiency |
| 6–12 reps | 65–85% | Hypertrophy (primary range) |
| 12–20 reps | 50–65% | Hypertrophy + muscular endurance |
| 20+ reps | <50% | Endurance, metabolic stress |

**Key finding:** Hypertrophy occurs across all rep ranges when sets are taken close to failure. A set of 30 reps to failure builds as much muscle as a set of 8 reps to failure (Schoenfeld et al., 2017). Higher reps are more uncomfortable; lower reps are more technically demanding.

**Practical recommendation:** Use a mix. Heavy work (4–6 reps) for compound lifts, moderate rep work (8–12) for main movements, higher reps (12–20) for isolation exercises.

---

## 5. Compound vs. Isolation Movements

**Compound movements** (multi-joint): Bench press, squat, deadlift, overhead press, pull-up, row. Provide maximum mechanical tension, allow heaviest loads, greatest hormonal response. Should be the foundation of every session.

**Isolation movements** (single-joint): Curls, lateral raises, tricep pushdowns, leg curls. Target specific muscles, great for bringing up lagging body parts, useful for adding volume with less systemic fatigue.

**Recommended split:** 60–70% compound, 30–40% isolation by volume.

---

## 6. Key Exercises by Goal

**Chest:** Bench press (flat, incline, dumbbell), weighted dip, cable fly
**Back:** Deadlift, pull-up/lat pulldown, barbell/dumbbell row, cable row, face pull
**Legs:** Squat, Romanian deadlift, leg press, Bulgarian split squat, leg curl
**Shoulders:** Overhead press (barbell, dumbbell), lateral raise, rear delt fly
**Arms:** Barbell curl, hammer curl, skull crusher, tricep pushdown
**Core:** Ab wheel rollout, hanging leg raise, pallof press

**For running performance:** Prioritize squats, Romanian deadlifts, single-leg work (split squat, step-up), and calf raises. These directly improve running economy.

## 7. Training Splits

**Full Body (3x/week):**
- Best for: beginners, those with limited time, concurrent athletes (running + lifting)
- Hits each muscle 3x/week
- Lower volume per session but high frequency

**Upper/Lower (4x/week):**
- 2 upper body days, 2 lower body days
- Good balance of volume and frequency
- Excellent for concurrent training with running

**Push/Pull/Legs (6x/week or 3-day rotating):**
- Highest weekly volume per muscle group
- Requires more recovery capacity
- Can conflict with running schedule

**Recommendation for Dylan (runner + lifter):**
Upper/Lower split. Upper days can be done any day. Lower days need 48+ hours before/after a hard run.

## 8. Rest Periods

| Lift Type | Rest Period |
|-----------|------------|
| Heavy compound (1–5 reps) | 3–5 minutes |
| Moderate compound (6–12 reps) | 2–3 minutes |
| Isolation/accessory | 60–90 seconds |

Shorter rest periods increase metabolic stress but reduce mechanical tension on subsequent sets. For strength and hypertrophy, err toward longer rest on compound movements.

---

## 9. RPE — Rate of Perceived Exertion

Scale of 1–10. Used to autoregulate training based on daily readiness (WHOOP recovery).

| RPE | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| 6 | Could do 4+ more reps |
| 7 | Could do 3 more reps |
| 8 | Could do 2 more reps |
| 9 | Could do 1 more rep |
| 10 | Absolute max — no more reps possible |

**Optimal training zone:** RPE 7–9 for most working sets. RPE 6 is too easy for hypertrophy stimulus. RPE 10 every set leads to overtraining.

**WHOOP integration:**
- Green recovery: Train at planned RPE (7–9)
- Yellow recovery: Reduce to RPE 6–7, drop 1 working set
- Red recovery: RPE 5–6 max, or skip lifting entirely

---

## 10. Deload Weeks

A planned week of reduced training to allow full systemic recovery and supercompensation.

**When to deload:**
- Every 4–6 weeks (planned)
- When WHOOP recovery is consistently yellow/red for 5+ days
- When strength stalls for 2+ weeks
- Before a major event (race, athletic competition)

**How to deload:**
- Reduce volume by 40–50% (same exercises, fewer sets)
- Keep intensity similar (same weight, just fewer sets)
- Do NOT do max effort attempts

## 11. Tracking Progression for Dylan's Lift Logs

When Dylan messages lifts (e.g., "bench 3x10 at 145"), CoachRex should track:
- Date, exercise, sets x reps x weight
- Flag when a personal record (PR) is set
- Note when same weight/reps is repeated (plateau signal)
- Suggest progression: if 3x10 at 145 feels easy → try 3x10 at 150 next session

**PR definition:** Any new best in weight, reps, or weight x reps combination for a given exercise.

**Plateau protocol:** If same weight/reps for 3+ consecutive sessions → change rep range, add a set, or try a variation.

## 12. Deload and Recovery in Context of Running

Running creates fatigue that competes with lifting recovery. Key considerations:

- **Leg day after a hard run:** Bad idea. At minimum 10 hours separation; ideally separate days.
- **Upper body after a run:** Generally fine — minimal interference.
- **Hard run after heavy squats/deadlifts:** Next-day leg soreness impairs running economy. Try to separate by 48 hours.
- **WHOOP strain from lifting:** WHOOP registers lifting as lower strain than equivalent cardio effort — but CNS fatigue from heavy lifting is real and affects recovery.

## 13. Common Mistakes That Limit Muscle Growth

1. **Not training near failure** — leaving too many reps in the tank (RPE 4–5 consistently)
2. **Insufficient protein** — muscle growth requires 0.7–1g/lb bodyweight daily
3. **Inconsistency** — skipping sessions erases the frequency advantage
4. **No progressive overload** — doing the same weights for months
5. **Too much volume too fast** — junk volume without adequate recovery
6. **Skipping compound lifts** — isolation-only training misses the primary stimulus
7. **Inadequate sleep** — 70% of daily growth hormone release occurs during deep sleep
8. **Overreaching** — chronic red WHOOP recovery suppresses muscle protein synthesis
9. **Poor exercise selection** — not training through full range of motion
10. **Not tracking** — can't progress what you don't measure
